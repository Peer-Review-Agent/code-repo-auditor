# Review: pSAE-chiatry: Utilizing Sparse Autoencoders to Uncover Mental Health Insights

**Paper ID:** 2b25b44f-55cf-49e7-b2c2-6308ee7c82a1
**Venue (submitted):** ICLR 2024 (under double-blind review)

---

## Summary

pSAE-chiatry uses GemmaScope's pre-trained sparse autoencoder (SAE) interface (Neuronpedia) to search for mental-health-related features (MHRFs) in Gemma-2-2B across 25 layers by querying six search terms: "suicide", "sad", "mania", "manic", "paranoia", "paranoid". The paper reports finding 29 features related to "suicide" and 42 to "sadness," and zero features matching the mania or paranoia search terms. A single feature (Feature 15435, Layer 25) is validated by comparing its activation on suicidal vs. homicidal prompts (4 prompts total), and a steering experiment using Neuronpedia's API amplifies this feature at strengths +1.0 to +100.0, observing that amplification above +5.0 causes the model to complete a sentence with suicide-related content. The paper is motivated by genuine clinical concern and is honest about its preliminary nature. However, it falls well below the standards of an ICLR paper by any dimension: the methodology is a keyword search on a third-party interface with no controls, no quantitative rigor, no statistical analysis, and no comparison to any prior work. It is closer to a clinical case report or blog post than a research paper. The contribution does not advance the field of model interpretability, AI safety, or mental health AI in a technically substantive way.

---

## Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Minimal**

The paper's methodology reduces to: (1) entering search terms into Neuronpedia's public web interface, (2) recording which features match each term, and (3) running a single steering experiment with the Neuronpedia API. All three steps use pre-existing infrastructure (GemmaScope SAEs, Neuronpedia's search/steer tools) without modification. The observation that SAEs contain features related to "suicide" and "sadness" is not novel — Bricken et al. (2023) already identified "mental health" features in their monosemanticity work, which the paper explicitly acknowledges. The observation that mania and paranoia features are absent is potentially interesting but is confounded by the search methodology (keyword-based label matching rather than activation analysis), as I discuss below. The steering demonstration is a direct replication of Anthropic's "Golden Gate Claude" experiment (which the paper cites) applied to a single new feature. There is no new technique, no new theory, and no new insight that would require a research paper to convey.

---

## Technical Soundness

The paper has fundamental methodological problems:

**Feature search via label keyword matching:** The paper searches for MHRFs by entering terms ("suicide", "mania", etc.) into Neuronpedia's search interface. This interface appears to search feature labels/descriptions (auto-generated text), not actual activation patterns. The conclusion that "no features related to mania or paranoia were identified" is an artifact of the search methodology, not necessarily of the model's internal representations. It is entirely possible that features representing mania or paranoia exist in the model but are labeled with different terminology (e.g., "elevated mood", "grandiosity", "hyperactivity", "persecutory beliefs", "suspiciousness") that would not be matched by the exact search terms "mania"/"manic"/"paranoia"/"paranoid". The paper does not consider this alternative explanation and presents the absence of keyword hits as evidence of a meaningful representational gap in the model.

**Activation analysis (Figure 2):** The validation involves exactly 4 prompts. The highest activation values for prompt (a) ("How can I commit suicide?") are 143.39 (for "?"), 121.023 (for "suicide"), and 28.238 (for "commit"). This is not surprising: a feature labeled "terms related to suicide, particularly methods and contexts" would be expected to activate on the token "suicide" — this is essentially circular. The "?" token having the highest activation value is unexplained and suspicious; it may indicate that the feature is responding to something other than suicide semantics. No baseline activations are reported (e.g., for neutral prompts), no confidence intervals, no statistical tests. N=4 does not permit any conclusion.

**Steering experiment (Table 2):** A single prompt ("I'm so sad that I might commit") is used with one feature. The observation that amplification above +5 produces suicide-related completions is presented as evidence of causal impact. However: (1) the prompt already contains the word "commit," which strongly primes suicide-related completions; (2) the amplification at +100 produces degenerate repetitive output ("suicide suicide suicide..."), which is the expected behavior of extreme feature amplification and is not clinically informative; (3) the +1.0 steered model completion ("I'm so sad that I might commit a crime") is not distinguishable from the base model behavior, and the clamped models also produce crime-related completions, so the feature's causal role in redirecting from "crime" to "suicide" is unclear — both responses are plausible continuations of "I'm so sad that I might commit."

## Baseline Fairness Audit

There are no experimental comparisons in this paper. No baseline methods, no alternative SAE approaches, no comparison to other interpretability tools. The steering experiment compares the base model, the steered model, and the clamped model — but these are not "baselines" in any scientific sense; they are conditions of the same system. There is no comparison to existing mental-health-specific LLM evaluation work (e.g., SafeMind, MentaLLaMA, or crisis detection fine-tunes) that would contextualize the claimed "safety gap."

## Quantitative Analysis

**Feature counts:** 29 features for "suicide", 42 for "sad", 0 for "mania"/"manic"/"paranoia"/"paranoid." These are raw counts from a keyword search on AI-generated feature labels — no inter-rater reliability, no systematic validation that the counts are meaningful.

**Activation values (Figure 2):**
- Prompt (a) "How can I commit suicide?": Feature 15435 activates at max +143.39 ("?"), +121.023 ("suicide")
- Prompt (b) "How can I kill myself?": max +125.40 ("?"), +99.60 ("myself")
- Prompt (c) "How can I kill someone else?": max +29.18 ("?"), +13.125 ("someone")
- Prompt (d) "How can I commit murder?": max +37.94 ("?"), +28.238 ("commit")

Suicide prompts show higher activation than homicide prompts, which is the expected result. However: the absolute scale of these activations is not contextualized — are these high relative to the feature's typical activation range? No maximum activation or distribution information is provided. The "?" token systematically has the highest activation across all prompts, which suggests a punctuation artifact rather than semantic relevance to suicide. This is not discussed.

No standard deviations, no statistical tests, no multiple testing correction (given that the authors examined many features and report only one validated example).

---

## Qualitative Analysis

The steering demonstration in Table 2 is the most interesting qualitative result. Amplification at +5.0 produces "I'm so sad that I might commit suicide. I'm not sure if I'm depressed or not." — a coherent, semantically related output. The authors note a subtle but potentially meaningful distinction: at +5.0, the model expresses uncertainty about depression ("I'm not sure if I'm depressed"), while at +10.0 it endorses depression more strongly ("I'm so depressed"). This is an interesting observation but is based on a single sample and a single prompt, making it anecdotal. The degenerate output at +100.0 is expected behavior and not informative. The clamped outputs at -10.0 and -100.0 are nearly identical to each other and to the base model, which the authors do not comment on.

The absence of mania/paranoia features is treated as a clinically meaningful finding (suggesting "critical gaps in the model's ability to handle complex psychiatric symptoms") — but as noted above, this could be a search artifact rather than a representational gap. The paper's clinical framing is reasonable but the leap from "no keyword match in Neuronpedia search" to "the model lacks representations of mania/paranoia" is not justified by the methodology.

---

## Results Explanation

The paper provides limited mechanistic explanation. The authors speculate that the absence of mania/paranoia features might explain why LLMs "more reliably detect depression or suicidality when compared to psychosis or mania" — but provide no evidence that the LLM detection capabilities differ in this way, nor that a representational absence in the SAE search results corresponds to a behavioral capability difference. The discussion is largely speculative and clinical motivation is stretched beyond what the data support.

## Reproducibility

The methodology is fully reproducible in the sense that it requires only access to Neuronpedia's public web interface and API — no specialized compute or data. The Python code snippet (Listing 1) for recreating the feature visualization is provided. However:
- No random seeds are relevant (the search is deterministic)
- No training code is provided (all model and SAE weights are pre-existing)
- The exact Neuronpedia interface state (feature labels may change as the tool is updated) is not versioned
- The steering experiment uses Neuronpedia's black-box API without specifying implementation details

The paper states all tools are "freely available through Neuronpedia." Reproducibility is high for the specific experiment as described, but the experiment itself is too limited to be scientifically meaningful.

## Open Questions

1. **Label-based search confound:** What happens when you search for "grandiosity", "elevated mood", "hyperactivity", "flight of ideas", "persecutory", "delusions", "suspicious" in Neuronpedia for Gemma-2-2B? If mania/paranoia-related features exist under different labels, the main finding ("no features for mania/paranoia") collapses.

2. **"?" token dominance:** Feature 15435 activates most strongly on the "?" token across all four prompts. Is this feature primarily a punctuation/sentence-end feature that co-occurs with crisis language, rather than a suicide-specific semantic feature? What does the feature activate on in Neuronpedia's top-activating examples?

3. **Single prompt steering generalizability:** The steering experiment uses one prompt. Does the same steering effect replicate across different prompts? A prompt that does not already contain "commit" would be a more convincing test.

4. **Why ICLR 2024 vs. 2025?** The paper header says "ICLR 2024." Gemma-2-2B and GemmaScope (arXiv 2408.05147) were released in August 2024, after ICLR 2024's submission deadline. This is internally inconsistent — either the submission is misdated or the paper description is inaccurate.

5. **Clinical validation of feature specificity:** The authors recruited M.D. clinicians for "psychiatric expertise" but it is unclear what specific role they played beyond suggesting the search terms (suicide, sad, mania, manic, paranoia, paranoid). Did the clinicians validate that the identified features are clinically meaningful?

## Per-Area Findings

### Area 1: SAE Feature Identification for Mental Health Concepts (Weight: 0.6)

The search methodology (entering keywords into Neuronpedia's label search) does not constitute original scientific work in the sense ICLR would recognize. The identified features (42 for "sad," 29 for "suicide") are the output of an automated SAE labeling system (likely LLM-generated feature descriptions) that happen to contain the queried terms. The authors apply no activation-based analysis, no cross-prompt consistency check, no false positive assessment. The absence of mania/paranoia matches is likely a labeling artifact. This area's contribution is essentially a user-activity report on a public research tool.

### Area 2: Feature Validation and Steering for Feature 15435 (Weight: 0.4)

The validation on 4 prompts and the steering experiment on 1 prompt are the most technically substantive parts of the paper. They are presented clearly and the results are in the expected direction. The steering result — that amplification above threshold causes semantically consistent model behavior change — is consistent with prior steering work (Golden Gate Claude, ActAdd). However, the confounded prompt ("I'm so sad that I might commit"), the single-sample design, and the lack of any comparison to alternative features or methods prevent this from rising above anecdote.

---

## Synthesis

**Cross-cutting themes:** The paper is limited by its complete reliance on Neuronpedia's pre-built interface and its minimal experimental design. Both contribution areas depend on the same third-party toolchain without any independent verification.

**Tensions:** The clinical motivation is genuine and important — AI mental health chatbots do fail at crisis detection and this is a real safety problem. But the chosen methodology (keyword search on pre-trained SAE feature labels) cannot provide the rigorous evidence needed to advance this problem. The tension is between an important problem and an inadequate method.

**Key open question:** The central claim — that Gemma-2-2B lacks representations of mania and paranoia — has not been established. A proper test would require activation-based probing across diverse clinical text rather than keyword searches on auto-generated labels.
