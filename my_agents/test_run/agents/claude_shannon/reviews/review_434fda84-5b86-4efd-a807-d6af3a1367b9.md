# Review: Erase or Hide? Suppressing Spurious Unlearning Neurons for Robust Machine Unlearning

**Paper ID:** 434fda84-5b86-4efd-a807-d6af3a1367b9
**Venue:** ICLR 2026 (published)

---

## Summary

This paper identifies a failure mode in LLM machine unlearning they call "shallow alignment": existing methods (Gradient Ascent, Gradient Difference, DPO, NPO, RMU) introduce spurious unlearning neurons that suppress display of target knowledge rather than erasing the underlying representations. The diagnostic is an attribution-based analysis (neuron influence scores) showing that negative influence increases more than positive influence decreases after unlearning. Based on this diagnosis, they propose SSIUU, which adds an L2 regularization term to prevent growth of negative attribution during training. Experiments on FaithUn and TOFU with Llama-3.2-3B and Qwen-2.5-3B show that SSIUU better resists two retraining attack scenarios: harmful (fine-tuning on a small fraction of the forget set) and benign (fine-tuning on Alpaca). The paper is technically solid, the diagnostic framing is genuinely useful, and the ICLR acceptance is well-earned. The main weakness is that SSIUU's regularization adds significant per-step computational cost (attribution computation per training step) and the approach is tested only on small (3B) models and two datasets.

---

## Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Substantial**

The core conceptual contribution — demonstrating that existing unlearning methods increase negative neuron influence rather than decreasing positive influence, and naming this "shallow alignment" via "spurious unlearning neurons" — is a genuine diagnostic advance. Prior work (Deeb & Roger 2024, Hu et al. 2024) demonstrated that unlearned knowledge can be recovered via retraining, but did not identify the underlying mechanistic cause in terms of attribution dynamics. The attribution analysis framework (building on Yang et al. 2023) applied to the unlearning context is the paper's most original contribution.

SSIUU itself is a straightforward regularization: compute attribution scores at each step, penalize growth of negative attribution via L2. This is engineering-level application of an existing idea. The combination of diagnostic + regularization is coherent and well-motivated.

Prior closest work: Deeb & Roger (2024) and Hu et al. (2024) showed retraining vulnerability without attribution analysis. KLUE (Yang et al. 2025) improves unlearning robustness via preference optimization — included as a baseline, which is appropriate.

## Technical Soundness

The attribution formula (Equation 1) is `A = h * (∂P/∂h)` — a gradient × activation product, which is the integrated gradients / input-gradient attribution approximation. This is a well-established technique. The paper applies it element-wise to transformer neurons, tracking changes in positive vs. negative attribution before/after unlearning. The formulation is internally consistent.

The SSIUU objective (Equation 3) penalizes changes in `A_θ_{t-1}^i` for neurons with negative attribution. One important detail: the paper states "we prevent gradients from flowing through `A_{θ_{t-1}}`" — treating the previous-step attribution as a constant. This is necessary for computational tractability but means the regularization is approximating a more principled objective. The paper is transparent about this.

Concern: The attribution scores are computed per-token (Equation 1 involves `h_{i,k}` for token position k) but then aggregated via max for D+ and min for D- (Equation 2). This max/min aggregation is not theoretically motivated — why is the worst-case token the right summary statistic? The paper should discuss whether mean or sum aggregation would change the conclusions.

The early-stopping criterion based on forget set score reaching a threshold (0.33 for FaithUn, ROUGE-L = τ for TOFU) is reasonable but introduces a confound: methods that converge faster may have less opportunity to develop spurious neurons. SSIUU's convergence speed relative to baselines is not reported.

## Baseline Fairness Audit

**Included baselines:** GA, GD, DPO, NPO, RMU, KLUE — this is a comprehensive set covering gradient-based, preference-based, and representation-based approaches.

**Fairness concerns:**
1. SSIUU uses GD as its backbone (Lθt in Equation 3). The comparison to standalone GD is thus informative — SSIUU is GD + regularization. This should be stated more prominently as the primary baseline.
2. All methods use the same early stopping criterion (forget set threshold), but the paper does not report how many gradient steps each method takes to converge. If SSIUU requires more steps (due to the attribution computation overhead), it may have a fairness advantage from additional exposure to the retain set.
3. NPO fails to converge on TOFU and is excluded — this is noted in a footnote. The exclusion is appropriate if the method fails, but its failure mode should be analyzed rather than silently dropped.
4. KLUE is included on FaithUn but the method citation (Yang et al. 2025) is the FaithUn paper itself, which is the primary benchmark — this circular relationship between benchmark and baseline is worth noting but not a fatal flaw.

## Quantitative Analysis

**FaithUn results (Table 1, Llama-3.2):**
- All methods achieve FS = 0.0 after unlearning, confirming basic unlearning success.
- Harmful attack (p=0.1): SSIUU 14.81% vs. GA 68.42%, GD 48.13%, NPO 18.33%, RMU 52.63%. SSIUU is best. Gap to NPO is small (14.81 vs. 18.33).
- Harmful attack (p=0.3): SSIUU 14.29% vs. GA 73.33%, GD 54.76%, NPO 18.75%, RMU 75.53%. SSIUU best.
- Benign attack: SSIUU 13.33% vs. DPO 15.34%, GA 16.71%, NPO 18.62%. SSIUU best but DPO and GA are close (15.34 and 16.71).
- Retention Score (RS): SSIUU 84.70 vs. Default 89.22. GD 81.03. SSIUU maintains RS better than most, but loses ~4.5 points from default vs. GD losing ~8 points.
- Utility Score (US): SSIUU 56.28 vs. Default 57.40. Minimal degradation, consistent with other methods.

**FaithUn results (Qwen-2.5):** Consistent pattern — SSIUU dominant on attacks (4.76% harmful p=0.1, 29.41% p=0.3, 13.04% benign).

**TOFU results (Table 2, Llama-3.2):** SSIUU achieves FS=17.75 (not fully unlearned at threshold, unlike FaithUn where FS=0) but dominates on attacks (31.82% and 37.53% vs. GA 48.72%/54.21%, DPO 87.39%/88.69%). Retention (92.74) and Utility (91.67) preserved well.

**Statistical reporting:** The paper states three runs per attack scenario and reports means. Standard deviations are not shown in Tables 1-2 — this is a gap. Given that attack scores vary (e.g., SSIUU 14.81 vs. NPO 18.33 on FaithUn Llama p=0.1), error bars would confirm statistical significance of SSIUU's advantage over NPO in some conditions.

**Attribution correlation (Figure 6b):** SSIUU achieves ρ=0.99 (correlation of attribution distributions before/after attack) vs. GA ρ=0.73, GD ~0.87, NPO ~0.87. This is a compelling supplementary analysis.

## Qualitative Analysis

The logit lens analysis (Figure 4) is an excellent supplementary result. GD pushes intermediate-layer representations far below chance level (0.2 accuracy at layers 18–27), indicating over-unlearning that creates an abnormal representational pattern that is fragile. SSIUU's layer-wise accuracy converges to ~0.5 (chance) uniformly — the target knowledge is genuinely removed without over-compensation.

Figure 5 (influence variation by module/layer) shows that SSIUU spreads positive influence removal across layers, while GD concentrates it in the last layers. This is interpretable: GD modifies late-layer representations but preserves earlier knowledge encodings. SSIUU's attribution regularization forces the unlearning to propagate deeper. The finding that spurious neurons concentrate in Attention Q and K modules is novel and mechanistically interesting.

The method appears to have a potential failure mode when the forget set is small (p=0.01 for TOFU) — the TOFU forget set threshold is ROUGE-L based, and SSIUU achieves FS=17.75 (not zero), suggesting partial forgetting. This is acceptable since TOFU uses a stringent threshold, but it suggests SSIUU's regularization may sometimes prevent full forgetting.

---

## Results Explanation

The paper provides good mechanistic explanations for most results. The key mechanism — that SSIUU's regularization prevents negative influence growth, forcing the unlearning to reduce positive influence instead — is directly supported by Figures 3, 5, and 6. The correlation analysis in Figure 6b provides a clean story: SSIUU preserves attribution structure, while other methods create unstable attribution landscapes that retraining disrupts.

What is unexplained: (1) Why does SSIUU still show some vulnerability to the p=0.3 harmful attack on TOFU (37.53%)? The paper doesn't analyze this. (2) The Qwen-2.5 p=0.3 result for SSIUU (29.41%) is much worse than Llama-3.2 (14.29%) — model architecture differences are not investigated. (3) The paper does not explain why DPO performs well on benign attacks (15.34 for Llama FaithUn, close to SSIUU's 13.33) but poorly on harmful attacks.

## Open Questions

1. The paper tests only 3B models. Would the spurious unlearning neuron phenomenon be more or less pronounced in larger models (7B, 13B, 70B)? At larger scale, the redundancy of knowledge encoding may mean the negative influence suppression strategy works longer before retraining disrupts it.

2. SSIUU uses GD as its backbone. Would the same regularization applied to NPO (which already shows better robustness than GD) produce further improvements, suggesting the regularization is orthogonal to the base method?

3. The attribution regularization is computed per-step on the forget set. What is the computational overhead per training step? If attribution computation adds 2-3× per-step cost, the comparison is not purely about method quality.

4. The paper excludes NPO on TOFU due to failure to converge. What specifically causes this failure? If SSIUU prevents NPO-style over-unlearning, does SSIUU+NPO backbone converge stably on TOFU?

5. The benign attack uses 1,000 Alpaca instances. How sensitive are the results to the choice of benign dataset? Would other instruction-following benchmarks (e.g., ShareGPT, OpenHermes) produce the same vulnerability pattern in baselines?
